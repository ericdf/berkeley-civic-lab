Annotated Readings

No Way to Run a City

A library of annotated readings of real public documents — budgets, letters, proposals, and announcements — that illustrate how important decisions are framed, and what gets left outside the frame.

The goal is not to determine whether any policy is right. The goal is to practice recognizing framing, identifying omitted alternatives, and asking the questions a disciplined process should answer before decisions are made.

Featured Analysis

Urgent Budget Update from Mayor Ishii: Affordable Housing at Risk

Mayor Adena Ishii  ·  June 23, 2026  ·  Email to constituents

Hours before Berkeley's final budget vote, residents received an urgent email encouraging them to contact councilmembers in support of one side of a contested housing funding decision.

Whether one agrees with the proposal is almost beside the point.

The remarkable question is procedural. A decision involving millions of public dollars, competing affordable housing strategies, and the future of who gets to live in Berkeley was reduced, in its final hours, to a rallying cry asking supporters to flood the zone before the vote. Public participation is essential. Good budgeting, however, should not depend on whichever proposal generates the strongest eleventh-hour political response.

This analysis asks whether the underlying decision was presented in a way that enabled residents to compare competing alternatives — or primarily encouraged them to mobilize behind one.

Outside the Frame

  • The full range of options under Measure U1
From: Mayor Adena Ishii <mayor@berkeleyca.gov>
Date: Tuesday, June 23, 2026
Subject: Urgent Budget Update from Mayor Ishii: Affordable Housing at Risk
Statement from Mayor Ishii Regarding Affordable Housing Funding
and the 2027–2028 Budget Proposals
Dear Community Member:

There is an important vote to pass the City of Berkeley's budget tomorrow, and I want to highlight an important issue for you. A new proposal submitted by Councilmembers Kesarwani, Blackaby, and Taplin removes funding for Small Sites and uses Measure U1 (funding for affordable housing) to cover unallocated positions in the city's general fund.

The voters of Berkeley approved Measure U1 in 2016 by an overwhelming majority. The intention of the tax is to raise funds for affordable housing and homeless services. The Housing Advisory Commission (HAC) requested that Council allocate $5 million for small sites. This new proposal from Councilmembers Kesarwani, Blackaby, and Taplin is in direct contradiction of the recommendation from the HAC — allocating $0 to small sites.

There are two main reasons I disagree with this proposal:

1) Small Sites helps prevent displacement

Today, Berkeleyside released an article, "Should the city of Berkeley help buy these affordable apartments?", highlighting a Small Sites project that could be funded by my budget proposal. It talked about the importance of preserving affordable housing and preventing displacement. My budget proposal would provide funding to support Small Sites.

2) Accountability and Transparency Matter

As Mayor, accountability and transparency have been central to my work, and as the former President of the League of Women Voters, I know that this is a good governance issue. If we pass a tax, we need to ensure that the funding goes to its intended purposes. We want the voters and our residents to trust us.

What can you do?

I encourage everyone to take a moment and write to your Councilmembers (council@berkeleyca.gov), come to the council meeting, or participate in the meeting online. If you agree with me, thank Councilmembers Humert [sic], Tregub, and O'Keefe for their support and encourage the other Councilmembers to support my budget.

Tomorrow's meeting takes place at 6:00 pm at the BUSD Board Room, 1231 Addison Street.

In community,

Adena Ishii
Berkeley Mayor

The subject line establishes the frame before the first sentence is read: housing is "at risk." The reader is invited to respond to urgency rather than compare alternatives.
The proposal is described as removing funding and redirecting tax revenue — framing the alternative as a subtraction. The question not asked: what does the alternative proposal fund, and at what tradeoff?
Voter authorization establishes that the tax is legitimate. It does not by itself determine which affordable housing strategy — preservation, production, or services — should receive priority this cycle. Authorization is not prioritization.
Displacement prevention is a real and measurable harm. The framing focuses on one group of potential beneficiaries — current tenants in at-risk buildings. Not in the frame: households that might be housed by new production, or how many households each strategy helps per dollar.
"Accountability" is invoked to mean: fund the purpose voters intended. This is legitimate. It also forecloses the question of whether the original allocation — Small Sites — is the highest and best use of the funds compared to other eligible purposes within Measure U1's scope.
Public participation is valuable. The ask here is specifically to mobilize supporters of one position before a vote scheduled for the next day. The question a disciplined process raises: was there an earlier opportunity for residents to compare both proposals with full information before the final hours?
The email identifies which councilmembers to thank and which to pressure. This is political communication, not neutral information. That is not a criticism — elected officials communicate politically. It is worth noting when reading public communications as budget analysis.

Questions Still Unanswered

The annotated document above does not reach conclusions. These are the questions that remain.

  1. How many households would Small Sites funding preserve, at what cost per household?
  2. How many households could the same dollars house through new production?
  3. What other uses of Measure U1 funds were considered and rejected, and why?
  4. What did the alternative proposal fund, and what was the reasoning of the councilmembers who supported it?
  5. Was there a public process in which residents could compare both proposals before the final evening?
  6. What is the long-term fiscal effect of each strategy on the city's property-tax base and service obligations?
Good budgeting does not eliminate disagreement. It ensures disagreements are resolved by comparing alternatives, examining tradeoffs, and answering difficult questions — not by whichever proposal generates the greatest political momentum immediately before the vote.
