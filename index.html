A Budget Is Not a List of Good Things

It is a ranking.

Every dollar spent on one thing is a dollar not spent on something else. Scarcity is not a failure of will — it is the condition that makes priorities necessary.

Six Questions Every Dollar Must Answer

Every program that wants public funding should earn its place. The framework asks these six questions of every budget proposal.

1

Is This a Core City Responsibility?

Distinguish what the city must do from what it has chosen to do. Public safety and infrastructure are obligations. Arts support is a choice. Choices face a higher burden of proof when competing with obligations.

Example test: Is this something Berkeley must provide, or something Berkeley has chosen to provide?
2

Is It More Important Than Competing Needs?

Every dollar has an opportunity cost. What are we not doing to pay for this?

Example test: What would we have to cut or delay to pay for this?
3

Does It Work — and How Would We Know?

Good intentions aren't enough. Expect measurable results and regular, honest evaluation.

Example test: What is the outcome? How will we measure it?
4

Does Outside Funding Actually Reduce Our Burden?

Free money isn't free if it creates ongoing costs, obligations, service-level effects, or harder exit costs.

Example test: Will this leave Berkeley owning the problem long-term?
5

Who Else Should Be Solving or Sharing This?

If a need is regional or shared, the solution and the cost should be shared too — not borne by Berkeley alone.

Example test: Is Berkeley solving a regional problem with local dollars?
6

When Do We Stop, Shrink, or Redesign It?

Funding something once should not create a permanent entitlement. Every program needs a sunset or renewal process.

Example test: What's the exit date? What causes us to scale back?

Free money is not free if Berkeley owns the consequences.

Ongoing Costs

Grants end. Staff, programs, and facilities funded by outside money often remain — paid for by Berkeley.

Service-Level Obligations

Accepting a grant may create legal or community expectations that outlast the funding itself.

Harder Exit Costs

Winding down a funded program is often more expensive — politically and financially — than never starting it.

Funding Once Is Not a Lifetime Appointment.

Every program should have a renewal process: measured, compared, and decided on the evidence.

  • Precedent
  • Political pressure

  • One-time fixes

After: What Should Drive Decisions

  • Stated priorities
  • Evidence of effectiveness
  • Full lifecycle costs
  • Transparency
  • Sunset review

Statement of Philosophy

The purpose of this framework is not to predetermine winners and losers. Its purpose is to ensure every proposal is evaluated using the same transparent standards.

Reasonable people can disagree about individual budget decisions while agreeing that every public dollar deserves the same careful review.

Endorse the Berkeley Budget Discipline Framework

By endorsing this framework you are supporting the decision-making framework — not every future application of it. Different people may reasonably reach different conclusions when applying these principles to individual budget decisions.
