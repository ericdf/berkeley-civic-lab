The Berkeley Budget Discipline Framework

This framework does not begin by naming cuts. It begins by naming standards.

Every budget proposal — regardless of its source or constituency — should answer the same six questions. One standard for every public dollar.

1

Is This the City's Responsibility?

Not all public expenditures carry the same weight. It is useful to distinguish among three categories:

  • Legal obligations: debt service, pension contributions, court judgments, and employee compensation required by contracts. These must be met.
  • Core public services: police, fire, streets, libraries, parks, and basic administration. Residents have a reasonable right to expect these from their city government.
  • Discretionary policy choices: financial support for arts organizations, grant programs, pilot initiatives, and property acquisitions beyond core operational needs. These may be worthwhile, but they are choices — not obligations.

This distinction matters when budgets are tight. Discretionary choices face a higher burden of proof when competing for the same dollars as legal obligations and core services. A city that treats everything as equally obligatory has no principled basis for making hard tradeoffs.

Example test: Is this a legal obligation, a core public service, or a discretionary policy choice? If discretionary, how does it rank against unfunded obligations?

2

Is It More Important Than Competing Needs?

Every dollar spent here is a dollar that cannot be spent somewhere else. Choosing one priority means choosing not to fund another. Good budgeting makes those tradeoffs explicit.

The question is not whether a program is worthy — it is whether it is more worthy than the alternatives it displaces. Presenting proposals in isolation, without naming what they replace, obscures real tradeoffs. The framework requires that every addition be weighed against real alternatives.

Example test: What would we have to cut, defer, or forgo to fund this? Is that tradeoff explicit in the proposal?

3

Does It Work — and How Would We Know?

Good intentions are not outcomes. Public dollars should be directed toward programs with demonstrated effectiveness or, for new programs, clear and measurable goals that allow honest evaluation. The expectation of accountability is not a bureaucratic burden — it is the basic respect owed to taxpayers.

Financial accountability and program accountability are different. Financial audits verify that money was spent as reported. They generally do not determine whether a program achieved its intended outcomes. Both matter, but they answer different questions.

Programs that cannot define their outcomes cannot be evaluated. Programs that cannot be evaluated cannot be improved or ended. The framework requires measurable results and regular evaluation built into every funding decision.

Accountability questions: What outcome was promised? How will success be measured? Who is accountable for reporting results? What happens if the program falls short?

4

Does Outside Funding Actually Reduce Our Burden?

Grants, state and federal funds, and private contributions appear as benefits with no cost. But accepting outside funding often creates obligations that outlast the funding itself: ongoing staffing costs, service-level expectations, administrative overhead, and political difficulty in unwinding programs once started.

The full cost of accepting outside money must be counted, including the lifecycle costs that Berkeley will own when the external funding ends. "Free money" that turns into a permanent local obligation is not free.

Example test: What does Berkeley own when this funding ends? What are the ongoing costs — staff, facilities, services — that will need local dollars?

5

Who Else Should Be Solving or Sharing This?

Some problems are genuinely regional or shared — experienced by multiple jurisdictions, benefiting people across municipal boundaries, or caused by actors beyond Berkeley's control. When that is the case, the appropriate response is a shared solution, not a unilateral Berkeley expenditure that subsidizes neighboring jurisdictions who contribute nothing.

There is a subtler risk beyond simple cost-sharing: Berkeley can end up serving a regional need with mostly local dollars. When one city builds substantially more capacity than neighboring jurisdictions, people in need may be directed there formally or informally. That does not make the investment wrong, but it does mean Berkeley should ask what regional partners are contributing, what limits exist, and whether local taxpayers are being asked to sustain a burden that extends beyond city boundaries.

This is not an argument against compassion or investment. It is an argument for regional coordination. Berkeley acting alone may feel like leadership but produce worse regional outcomes than Berkeley insisting on shared responsibility.

Example test: Is this a Berkeley problem or a regional problem? If Berkeley invests heavily here, does that reduce pressure on neighboring cities to act — making Berkeley a destination rather than solving the underlying problem?

6

When Should This Be Reviewed, Renewed, Redesigned, or Concluded?

Funding a program once should not create open-ended obligations. Budgets that grow only through addition — never through evaluation and discontinuation — eventually become unsustainable.

Different kinds of public programs require different review cycles. A pilot should have a defined evaluation and decision point. An ongoing public service should be reviewed periodically for performance, efficiency, and continued public value. The important principle is not that every program expires automatically — it is that no program should continue indefinitely without meaningful review.

Sunset review is not punishment for programs that work. It is principled stewardship applied to all programs equally — the same standard the framework demands at the beginning of a program's life, applied at every renewal.

Example test: What is the review date? Under what conditions would we scale back, restructure, or conclude this program?

One Standard for Every Public Dollar

Reasonable people can disagree about individual budget decisions while agreeing on process. This framework is the process — not the answer, but the discipline that makes better answers possible.

Good programs should welcome good scrutiny. Standards before examples. Principled decision-making before political pressure. Evidence before conclusions.
