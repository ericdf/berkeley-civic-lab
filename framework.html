The Berkeley Budget Discipline Framework

This framework does not begin by naming cuts. It begins by naming standards.

Every budget proposal — regardless of its source or constituency — should answer the same six questions. One standard for every public dollar.

1

Is This a Core City Responsibility?

There is a meaningful difference between what a city must do and what a city has chosen to do. Public safety, infrastructure, and basic public health are obligations — things residents have a reasonable right to expect from their city government. Arts funding, social programs, and economic development initiatives may be worthwhile, but they are choices, not obligations.

This distinction matters when budgets are tight. Chosen expenditures face a higher burden of proof when competing for the same dollars as core obligations. A city that treats everything as equally obligatory has no basis for making hard tradeoffs — and will protect popular choices at the expense of fundamental duties.

Example test: Is this something Berkeley must provide, or something Berkeley has chosen to provide? If the latter, how does it rank against unfunded obligations?

2

Is It More Important Than Competing Needs?

Every dollar spent on one thing is a dollar not spent on something else. This is not a philosophical observation — it is the operating condition of every municipal budget. The question is not whether a program is worthy; the question is whether it is more worthy than the alternatives it displaces.

Presenting proposals in isolation, without explicitly naming what they replace, obscures real tradeoffs. The framework requires that every addition be weighed against real alternatives.

Example test: What would we have to cut, defer, or forgo to fund this? Is that tradeoff explicit in the proposal?

3

Does It Work — and How Would We Know?

Good intentions are not outcomes. Public dollars should be directed toward programs with demonstrated effectiveness or, for new programs, clear and measurable goals that allow honest evaluation. The expectation of accountability is not a bureaucratic burden — it is the basic respect owed to taxpayers.

Programs that cannot define their outcomes cannot be evaluated. Programs that cannot be evaluated cannot be improved or ended. The framework requires measurable results and regular evaluation built into every funding decision.

Example test: What is the specific, measurable outcome? By when? How will we evaluate it — and what happens if the evidence is disappointing?

4

Does Outside Funding Actually Reduce Our Burden?

Grants, state and federal funds, and private contributions appear as benefits with no cost. But accepting outside funding often creates obligations that outlast the funding itself: ongoing staffing costs, service-level expectations, administrative overhead, and political difficulty in unwinding programs once started.

The full cost of accepting outside money must be counted, including the lifecycle costs that Berkeley will own when the external funding ends. "Free money" that turns into a permanent local obligation is not free.

Example test: What does Berkeley own when this funding ends? What are the ongoing costs — staff, facilities, services — that will need local dollars?

5

Who Else Should Be Solving or Sharing This?

Some problems are genuinely regional or shared — experienced by multiple jurisdictions, benefiting people across municipal boundaries, or caused by actors beyond Berkeley's control. When that is the case, the appropriate response is a shared solution, not a unilateral Berkeley expenditure that subsidizes neighboring jurisdictions who contribute nothing.

There is a subtler risk beyond simple cost-sharing: Berkeley can become a regional destination precisely because it invests more than its neighbors. A city that builds more homeless services, more shelter beds, more supportive housing than surrounding cities does not just serve its own unhoused residents — it signals to neighboring jurisdictions that they need not build comparable capacity. The result is predictable: people in need are directed, formally or informally, toward Berkeley. Berkeley's generosity becomes a reason for neighboring cities to do less, and Berkeley absorbs a regional burden while others free-ride.

This is not an argument against compassion or investment. It is an argument for regional coordination. Berkeley acting alone may feel like leadership but produce worse regional outcomes than Berkeley insisting on shared responsibility.

Example test: Is this a Berkeley problem or a regional problem? If Berkeley invests heavily here, does that reduce pressure on neighboring cities to act — making Berkeley a destination rather than solving the underlying problem?

6

When Do We Stop, Shrink, or Redesign It?

Funding a program once should not create a permanent entitlement. Budgets that grow only through addition — never through evaluation and discontinuation — eventually become unsustainable. Every program should have either a sunset date or an explicit renewal process that requires a fresh affirmative decision, not mere inertia.

Sunset review is not punishment for programs that work. It is discipline applied to all programs equally — the same standard the framework demands at the beginning of a program's life, applied at every renewal.

Example test: What is the exit or review date? Under what conditions would we scale back, restructure, or end this program?

One Standard for Every Public Dollar

Reasonable people can disagree about individual budget decisions while agreeing on process. This framework is the process — not the answer, but the discipline that makes better answers possible.

Good programs should welcome good scrutiny. Standards before examples. Process before personalities. Evidence before conclusions.
