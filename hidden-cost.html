Hidden Costs

The Hidden Cost of Public Programs

Every program comes with two price tags: what it costs to start, and what it costs to own. Disciplined budgeting surfaces both.

Two Questions Every Program Budget Should Answer

A capital budget shows the first. The second is easier to miss.

Price Tag 1

What does it cost to start?

Capital appropriations, construction, design, setup, equipment purchases, and other one-time launch costs. This is the number that appears in budget headlines.

Price Tag 2

What does it cost to own?

Staffing, benefits, pensions, facilities, utilities, IT, equipment, maintenance, replacement cycles, insurance, and administration — year after year, long after the ribbon cutting.

Capacity also matters. Major projects also consume project management, legal, finance, procurement, HR, and executive attention — especially when departments are shrinking. But the core public-facing rule is simpler: show both price tags.

Case Study: The African American Holistic Resource Center

The AAHRC is used here not to argue for or against the project, but to show how hard it can be for residents to see the full cost of a public program.

Editorial principle: This analysis does not argue against the AAHRC or any specific program. It argues that disciplined budgeting requires surfacing both price tags — not only what it costs to start, but what it costs to own and sustain over time. That standard should apply to every program equally.

What Berkeley has clearly surfaced

The capital budget, building scope, construction cost, and funding sources are documented in public records.

What has not been equally surfaced

An earlier feasibility study estimated annual operating costs at roughly $1.5M–$2M. That estimate now likely needs updating given the expanded scope. The important point is not whether the old number is exactly right — it is that a current ownership model should be disclosed before residents are asked to judge affordability.

What does it cost to start?
  • When the project scope expanded from rehabilitating an existing structure to a 6,000 square foot new construction project, were the full downstream costs — ownership, staffing, operations — re-examined and disclosed?
  • What other programs or priorities lost funding as a result of this project's budget?
What does it cost to own?
  • What is the current annual operating cost estimate — and has it been updated since the scope changed?
  • How many City employees will be required to operate the center, and what are their benefits and pension obligations?
  • What are the ongoing facility costs — utilities, maintenance, trash collection, building systems, insurance?
  • What is the long-term General Fund commitment once any grants or one-time funds expire?
  • Can Berkeley afford to own and operate this for the next 30 years?
What does it cost to deliver?
  • Which departments are currently involved in developing it, and how many staff-hours are already invested?
  • Which existing work is being delayed because staff are assigned to this project?
If residents can see the capital budget but not the ownership model, they are seeing only one price tag.

No Major Program Should Move Forward on One Price Tag Alone

Before Berkeley starts, expands, or continues a significant program, residents should be able to see both price tags: what it costs to start, and what it costs to own.

1 The cost to start — capital, construction, design, and one-time launch
2 The cost to own — staffing, benefits, pensions, facilities, utilities, maintenance, IT, insurance, and administration, year after year

Every program comes with two price tags. Disciplined budgeting surfaces both before promises are made — not after obligations are locked in.
