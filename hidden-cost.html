Hidden Costs

The Hidden Cost of Public Programs

Disciplined budgeting surfaces all costs — not only what it takes to build something, but what it takes to own it and deliver it well.

Most public discussion stops at the visible tip. Disciplined budgeting requires naming everything below the waterline.

Three Questions Most Budgets Don't Ask

Public discussion usually stops at Question 1.

Question 1

What does it cost to build?

Capital appropriations, construction, design, and one-time setup. This is the number that appears in budget headlines.

Question 2

What does it cost to own?

Staffing, benefits, pensions, facilities, maintenance, replacement cycles, IT, and administration — year after year, long after the ribbon cutting.

Question 3

What does it cost to deliver?

The organizational capacity consumed: project management, legal, procurement, finance, HR, and executive attention — all finite and competed over.

Money Is Not the Only Scarce Resource

Every major initiative also consumes organizational capacity — the time, attention, and expertise of the people responsible for making it work. That capacity is finite and competed over by every project the city pursues simultaneously.

When departments are shrinking — through layoffs, vacancies, or budget reductions — every new initiative competes for the same implementation capacity that existing work already depends on. A city cannot add commitments without acknowledging what existing work is delayed or deprioritized as a result.

Case Study: The African American Holistic Resource Center

The AAHRC is used here not to argue for or against the project, but to demonstrate how difficult it is for residents to answer basic cost questions from public documents alone.

Editorial principle: This analysis does not argue against the AAHRC or any specific program. It argues that disciplined budgeting requires surfacing all costs — not only dollars, but also the long-term commitments and organizational capacity consumed by public decisions.

Questions residents cannot answer from public documents

  • How many City employees will ultimately support this center?
  • Which departments are currently involved in developing it?
  • How many staff-hours are already invested?
  • What recurring General Fund support will be required once it opens?
  • What benefits and pension obligations accompany City staffing of the center?
  • Which existing work is being delayed because staff are assigned to this project?
  • When the project scope expanded, what additional costs were considered and disclosed?
  • What other programs or priorities lost funding as a result of this project's budget?
If the answers to these questions cannot be assembled from public documents, that itself illustrates the framework. Residents cannot evaluate a project they cannot see the full cost of.

The Reusable Rule

Every significant public initiative should disclose three things before a funding decision is made. One standard, applied to every project equally.

1 Build Cost — the one-time capital and setup expenditure
2 Total Cost of Ownership — recurring costs across the program's life
3 Organizational Capacity Required — the staff time, expertise, and attention the project will consume

Only when all three are disclosed can residents and elected officials compare competing priorities fairly.
